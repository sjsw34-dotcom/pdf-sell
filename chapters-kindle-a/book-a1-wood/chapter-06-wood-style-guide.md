# Your Wood Style Guide

This is the chapter that doesn't exist in traditional Saju books. Because in the old texts, the Five Elements were about fate, fortune, and cosmic mechanics. Nobody was talking about what to wear.

But here's the thing: your element doesn't stop at your personality. It bleeds into everything — the colors you gravitate toward, the spaces you feel comfortable in, the clothes that make you feel like *yourself*, the food that satisfies you on a level that goes beyond taste.

This chapter is your practical guide to living as a Wood person — from your closet to your kitchen to your living room.

## Wood Color Palette

Every element has a color. Wood's is green. But "green" is a universe.

### Your Core Greens

**Forest green.** Deep, saturated, almost-black green. The color of pine needles in winter, of old leather-bound books, of a perfectly steeped matcha. This is Wood's power color — the one that makes you feel grounded and alive at the same time.

**Sage green.** Softer, more muted, with gray undertones. The color of dried herbs, of eucalyptus, of morning fog through trees. This is Wood's calming color — the one that brings your energy down without dampening it.

**Emerald green.** Bright, confident, unapologetic. The color of new growth after heavy rain, of a parrot's feather, of a jewel catching light. This is Wood's statement color — the one you wear when you want the world to notice your energy.

### Supporting Colors

Wood's palette isn't all green. Your supporting colors create depth:

**Black and charcoal.** Water feeds Wood. Dark, fluid colors anchor Wood's upward energy and add sophistication without competing with it.

**Brown and tan.** Earth tones ground Wood literally. The color of bark, of soil, of raw wood. These create a foundation for the greens to grow on.

**White and cream.** Used sparingly. A crisp white shirt under a forest green jacket. Cream walls in a room with green accents. White provides the contrast that makes Wood colors pop.

### Colors That Drain Wood

**Excessive red and orange.** Fire drains Wood's energy. A little warmth in your palette is fine — a terracotta accent, a russet scarf. But environments dominated by red and orange will gradually exhaust you.

**Cold metallics.** Silver, chrome, steel gray. Metal controls Wood. A sleek, all-chrome kitchen or a room full of steel furniture might look chic, but it'll feel subtly hostile to your element. If you like metallics, go for brass or gold-toned finishes instead — they carry warmth.

## Wood Wardrobe

Your element has a dress code. Not a rigid one — a resonance.

### Yang Wood Style

Yang Wood dresses the way they do everything else: with structure, intention, and an aversion to anything sloppy.

**The Yang Wood signature look:**
- Clean lines. Tailored fits. Structured shoulders.
- Quality over quantity — fewer pieces, better materials
- Classic rather than trendy. Yang Wood's wardrobe ages well because they don't chase fads.
- Dark, rich colors: forest green, navy, black, deep brown
- Details that signal precision: good leather, clean stitching, polished hardware
- Shoes matter. Yang Wood is often the person whose shoes are always clean.

**Yang Wood wardrobe essentials:**
- A perfectly fitted blazer in forest green or navy
- Dark denim or well-tailored trousers in a neutral
- A leather bag (backpack for casual, briefcase or tote for work) in brown or black
- One statement piece that says "I know exactly who I am" — a distinctive watch, a signature jacket, a ring they never take off
- White shirts. Several. Always crisp.

**What Yang Wood avoids:**
- Anything wrinkled, faded, or "intentionally distressed"
- Loud patterns that compete with their presence
- Fast fashion that falls apart after three washes
- Oversized, shapeless silhouettes (Yang Wood likes to see the body's structure)

### Yin Wood Style

Yin Wood dresses with more fluidity — more texture, more layers, more personality.

**The Yin Wood signature look:**
- Flowing fabrics. Layers that move.
- Patterns inspired by nature: florals (obviously), but also abstract organics, watercolors, botanical prints
- A mix of structured and soft — a tailored coat over a flowing dress, jeans with a silk top
- Colors that shift: olive, teal, moss, lavender, dusty rose
- Accessories that tell a story — vintage finds, handmade jewelry, pieces picked up while traveling
- Comfort is non-negotiable. Yin Wood won't sacrifice comfort for aesthetics, ever.

**Yin Wood wardrobe essentials:**
- A beautiful scarf. Silk, cashmere, or linen. Something that can transform a basic outfit.
- Layering pieces: cardigans, light jackets, open-front vests
- One pair of incredibly comfortable shoes in a neutral color
- A tote bag large enough to carry their entire life (because they will)
- Earthy jewelry: wooden beads, jade, turquoise, raw stone pieces

**What Yin Wood avoids:**
- Anything stiff or restrictive (tight collars, rigid fabrics, structured bodysuits)
- All-black everything (too stark, too final — Yin Wood needs color variation)
- Matching sets that are too coordinated (Yin Wood prefers curated imperfection)
- Synthetic fabrics that don't breathe (their body objects)

## Wood Home & Living Space

Your home should feel like an extension of your element. Here's how to create a space that recharges your Wood energy instead of depleting it.

### The Non-Negotiables

**Plants. Real ones.** This isn't decorative advice — it's elemental. Wood people in spaces without living greenery experience a low-grade energy drain that they might not consciously notice but absolutely feel. You don't need a jungle. Even one thriving plant on your desk changes the frequency of the room.

Start with something hard to kill (pothos, snake plant, ZZ plant) and work your way up. The act of caring for a plant — watching it grow, adjusting its position, noticing new leaves — is a form of meditation for Wood people.

**Natural light.** Wood's energy follows the sun. Spaces that are dark during daylight hours suppress your element. If you can't change your room's natural light, invest in full-spectrum light bulbs that simulate daylight. Position your desk near a window. Open the curtains before you open your laptop.

**Something wooden.** Not IKEA particleboard — actual wood. A wooden desk, a live-edge shelf, a cutting board hung on the wall, a driftwood sculpture, a wooden bowl on the table. The texture and warmth of real wood resonates with your element in a way that no other material does.

**A view of distance.** Wood energy rises and extends. A room with no view — no window, no depth, no sense of "beyond" — cramps Wood energy. If your space doesn't have a window with a view, create the illusion: a large photograph of a landscape, a mirror that extends the room's depth, or artwork that draws the eye outward.

### Room-by-Room Guide

**Bedroom:**
- Cool tones: sage green, muted blue, cream
- Natural fiber bedding: linen, cotton, bamboo (no synthetic sheets — your body objects during sleep)
- Minimal electronics visible from the bed
- One plant that thrives in low light (peace lily, fern)
- Blackout curtains (Wood sleeps better in complete darkness)

**Workspace:**
- Clear desk policy — clutter blocks Wood's directional thinking
- One healthy plant within arm's reach
- Natural light or warm-toned desk lamp (not cold fluorescent)
- Something green in your sightline: a green wall, a green mouse pad, a framed photo of trees
- A small object that represents your current goal (a vision board is too much — one object is enough)

**Kitchen:**
- Wooden cutting boards, wooden utensils, wooden bowl for fruit
- Herb garden on the windowsill (even just basil and mint)
- Green tea, matcha, or herbal teas visible and accessible
- Organized containers — Wood kitchens work best when everything has a place
- A good knife. Wood people develop almost spiritual relationships with their kitchen knives.

**Living room:**
- At least one substantial plant (a fiddle leaf fig, a monstera, a rubber tree)
- Natural materials: wood furniture, cotton or linen cushions, a wool throw
- Earth tones as a base with green accents
- A bookshelf (Wood people and books have a thing)
- Something tall — a floor lamp, a tall plant, a vertical piece of art. Vertical elements echo Wood's upward energy.

## Wood Scents & Atmosphere

Scent is one of the most powerful ways to connect with your element.

### Wood's Scents

**Tree-based scents:** Cedarwood, sandalwood, pine, cypress, juniper. These are Wood's home base. A cedarwood candle on your desk or a sandalwood incense stick in the evening can shift your energy more quickly than almost anything else.

**Herbaceous scents:** Rosemary, basil, thyme, eucalyptus, mint. Fresh, green, alive. These activate Wood's energy when it's sluggish.

**Citrus:** Lemon, bergamot, yuzu, grapefruit. Sour and bright, these align with Wood's flavor (sour) and provide a quick energy reset.

**Earth base notes:** Vetiver, patchouli, oakmoss. These ground Wood's upward energy and create a sense of stability.

### Creating Atmosphere

- Morning: herbaceous or citrus scents (activating)
- Work hours: subtle cedarwood or pine (focusing)
- Evening: sandalwood or vetiver (calming)
- Sleep: lavender with a drop of cedarwood (deeply calming for Wood types)

## Wood Food Culture

Beyond the nutritional advice in Chapter 5, Wood has a whole relationship with food *as culture* — how you eat, not just what you eat.

### The Wood Kitchen Philosophy

**Freshness over everything.** Wood people have an instinctive aversion to food that feels "dead." Heavily processed, long-shelf-life, industrially manufactured food tastes wrong to you — not just nutritionally but aesthetically. You want to see the aliveness in your food: the bright color of a fresh herb, the snap of a just-picked vegetable, the fizz of a naturally fermented drink.

**Seasonal eating comes naturally.** You probably already do this without thinking about it. In spring, you crave salads and lighter fare. In winter, you want soups and roasted root vegetables. This isn't a trend for you — it's a bodily instinct. Follow it.

**Cooking as creation.** Many Wood people find cooking to be one of their best stress-relief activities — not because it's relaxing (it's often not) but because it's *productive*. You start with raw ingredients and end with something complete. That arc — from nothing to something — satisfies Wood's builder instinct.

### Wood's Signature Ingredients

These are the ingredients that Wood people tend to reach for again and again:

- **Leafy greens** of every variety
- **Fresh herbs** — the ones you actually grow if given a windowsill
- **Citrus** — lemon on everything
- **Fermented foods** — kimchi, sauerkraut, kombucha, pickles
- **Green tea and matcha**
- **Nuts and seeds** — almonds, walnuts, pumpkin seeds
- **Avocado** (yes, the stereotype exists for a reason)
- **Olive oil** — preferably good quality, used generously
- **Sourdough** over commercial bread
- **Sprouts and microgreens** — baby plants. Of course.

### Wood's Ideal Meal

A meal that makes a Wood person feel *right*:
- A generous base of greens or vegetables
- A moderate portion of quality protein
- Something fermented or sour on the side
- A drizzle of good oil
- Fresh herbs as finishing, not afterthought
- A cup of green tea to close

Simple. Fresh. Structured. Alive. That's Wood food at its best.

## Bringing It All Together

Style, for a Wood person, isn't about following rules. It's about alignment.

When your wardrobe, your home, your food, and your environment all echo your elemental nature, something shifts. Not dramatically — subtly. You feel more like yourself. Decisions come easier. Stress has less grip. You sleep a little better, think a little clearer, move through the world with a little more ease.

You don't need to overhaul your entire life overnight. Start small. Add one plant. Wear more green. Buy a wooden cutting board. Squeeze lemon into your water.

Your element is already inside you. These are just ways of letting it express itself on the outside, too.
