# How You Negotiate

Every financial interaction involves a negotiation — explicit or implicit. The job interview. The freelance quote. The raise request. The vendor contract. The "let's split the bill" conversation. Even deciding whether to use a coupon.

Most people approach negotiation as if it's a single skill: ask confidently, hold firm, get the best deal. But negotiation isn't one skill — it's a dynamic that shifts depending on who's at the table. And your element determines how you show up at that table.

---

## Wood's Negotiation Style: The Closer

**Natural approach:** Wood negotiates by creating momentum. The deal has a direction, and Wood drives toward it. Wood's negotiation isn't about winning — it's about reaching the outcome efficiently.

**Strengths:** Speed and decisiveness. Wood doesn't linger in negotiation limbo. They make a clear proposal, address objections directly, and push for a decision. This confidence often catches the other party off guard — most people expect negotiation to involve extensive back-and-forth. Wood's directness can close deals faster than either party expected.

Wood is also excellent at framing negotiations in terms of future value. "This isn't just about the current rate — here's where this leads in twelve months." The growth narrative is Wood's most powerful negotiation tool.

**Weaknesses:** Impatience. Wood wants the deal done. This urgency can work against them when the other party realizes that Wood's eagerness to close means they'll accept less-than-optimal terms to avoid a prolonged process. Experienced negotiators exploit Wood's impatience by slowing things down.

Wood can also miss emotional dynamics. While Wood is focused on the logical trajectory of the deal, the other party might have emotional objections (fear, distrust, ego) that Wood's solution-oriented approach doesn't address.

**Wood's power move:** The walk-away. When Wood genuinely walks away from a deal — not as a tactic, but because the terms don't serve their growth plan — it creates enormous leverage. The key word is *genuinely*. Bluffing doesn't work for Wood because Wood's directness makes bluffs transparent.

**The negotiation sentence Wood needs to practice:** "I hear what you're saying. Tell me more about your concerns before I respond."

## Fire's Negotiation Style: The Charmer

**Natural approach:** Fire negotiates through relationship. Before terms are discussed, Fire has already established warmth, rapport, and a sense of shared energy. The other party *likes* Fire — and that liking creates latitude.

**Strengths:** Likability as leverage. People give better deals to people they like. This isn't manipulation — it's Fire being genuinely warm and the other party responding naturally. Fire's negotiations often feel less like negotiations and more like collaborative conversations, which produces better outcomes for both sides.

Fire is also exceptional at reading the room. They can sense when the other party is close to agreeing, when they're bluffing, and when they need reassurance. This emotional intelligence allows Fire to adjust their approach in real time.

**Weaknesses:** Discomfort with tension. Fire's relationship orientation means they'll sometimes sacrifice financial terms to preserve the vibe. Asking for more money feels like it might damage the connection — so Fire doesn't ask. This is especially costly in salary negotiations, where the initial awkwardness of a higher ask would be forgotten in a week but the financial impact lasts years.

Fire can also be talked into things through flattery. "You'd be so great at this" or "we really want YOU specifically" can make Fire agree to terms that don't actually reflect their value.

**Fire's power move:** Enthusiastic silence. After making a request, Fire should stop talking and radiate warm confidence. Fire's instinct is to fill silence with more words — but silence after a clear ask creates space for the other party to agree. The warmth stays. The pressure builds naturally.

**The negotiation sentence Fire needs to practice:** "I appreciate the offer. I need $X to make this work."

## Earth's Negotiation Style: The Accommodator

**Natural approach:** Earth negotiates by finding the middle ground. Earth's instinct is to understand what the other party needs and find a solution that works for everyone. This creates deals that stick — but doesn't always create deals that serve Earth fairly.

**Strengths:** Relationship preservation. Earth's negotiations never blow up. The other party always feels heard, respected, and fairly treated. This makes Earth excellent at long-term negotiations — contracts that renew, relationships that deepen, partnerships that compound over years.

Earth is also patient. Where Wood rushes and Fire charms, Earth waits. They can sit in uncomfortable negotiation dynamics without panicking, which sometimes produces better outcomes than aggressive tactics — the other party fills the patience with concessions.

**Weaknesses:** Chronic undervaluation. Earth's biggest financial vulnerability is negotiation. Earth genuinely believes that their needs should come second — and the other party's budget, constraints, and feelings matter more than Earth's own number. This produces a systematic pattern of undercharging, under-negotiating, and under-earning.

Earth also struggles with adversarial dynamics. When a negotiation becomes confrontational, Earth's instinct is to de-escalate by conceding. Aggressive negotiators learn this quickly and exploit it.

**Earth's power move:** Preparation. Earth negotiates poorly in the moment but excellently on paper. Before any negotiation, Earth should write down: (1) the minimum acceptable number, (2) the ideal number, and (3) the walk-away point. Then treat these as facts, not feelings. When the instinct to accommodate arises, the paper holds the line.

**The negotiation sentence Earth needs to practice:** "I value our relationship, and I also need to be compensated fairly. My rate is $X."

## Metal's Negotiation Style: The Analyst

**Natural approach:** Metal negotiates with data. Before the conversation begins, Metal has researched market rates, competitor pricing, industry benchmarks, and the other party's likely constraints. The negotiation isn't emotional — it's evidentiary.

**Strengths:** Unshakable preparation. Metal comes to the table knowing more than the other party expects. This creates an information advantage that's difficult to counter. When Metal says "the market rate for this is $X," they can cite five sources. This precision communicates competence and reduces the space for arbitrary pushback.

Metal is also immune to emotional manipulation. Flattery, urgency, social pressure — the tactics that work on Fire and Earth bounce off Metal completely. Metal evaluates the terms, not the feelings.

**Weaknesses:** Rigidity. Metal's commitment to the "correct" number can prevent creative deal-making. Sometimes the best deal isn't the number — it's the structure, the timeline, the additional benefits, the relationship value. Metal can win the negotiation and lose the opportunity by insisting on precision at the expense of flexibility.

Metal can also come across as cold in negotiations, which creates resistance. People don't just buy terms — they buy relationships. Metal's data-driven approach can make the other party feel like a line item rather than a partner.

**Metal's power move:** The structured compromise. "I understand your budget constraint. Here's a structure that meets your number while meeting my value: [creative alternative]." Metal's ability to engineer solutions from constraints is extraordinary when they choose to deploy it.

**The negotiation sentence Metal needs to practice:** "I see the data supports $X, and I'm open to discussing how we get there together."

## Water's Negotiation Style: The Empath

**Natural approach:** Water negotiates by understanding the other party's position so thoroughly that the deal practically constructs itself. Before Water makes an ask, they've already mapped the other party's constraints, motivations, fears, and unstated interests.

**Strengths:** Strategic empathy. Water's ability to see beneath the surface of what someone is saying gives them a negotiation advantage that's almost unfair. While the other party is presenting their position, Water is reading their actual position — which is often very different. This allows Water to make proposals that address what the other party really wants (but hasn't said), creating deals that feel like gifts.

Water is also excellent at reframing. When a negotiation stalls, Water can shift the conversation to a different level — from price to value, from cost to relationship, from transaction to partnership — in a way that unlocks movement.

**Weaknesses:** Avoidance. Water's deepest negotiation weakness is simply not showing up. The discomfort of asking for money — of putting a number on their value, of potentially hearing "no," of engaging in an explicitly transactional dynamic — causes Water to avoid negotiation entirely. They accept the first offer. They don't ask for raises. They price their services low to avoid the conversation.

Water can also over-empathize with the other party's constraints. "I understand their budget is tight" becomes a reason to lower the ask before it's even been made. Water negotiates against themselves.

**Water's power move:** Framing the ask as service. "I can deliver the most value at $X. Here's why that investment serves you." This converts the negotiation from "me asking for money" (which Water hates) to "me helping you get what you need" (which Water does naturally).

**The negotiation sentence Water needs to practice:** "My work is worth $X. I'm confident in that number."

## The Universal Negotiation Truth

Across all five elements, the most costly negotiation mistake is the same: not negotiating at all.

The raise you didn't ask for. The rate you didn't increase. The contract you signed without discussion. The first offer you accepted because pushing back felt uncomfortable.

Over a career, the difference between negotiating and not negotiating is hundreds of thousands of dollars. Not because any single negotiation is life-changing — but because the compound effect of consistently undervaluing yourself (or consistently capturing your value) is massive.

Your element gives you a style. Use it. But use it — don't skip the conversation because your style doesn't look like someone else's.
