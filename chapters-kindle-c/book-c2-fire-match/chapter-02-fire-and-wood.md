# Fire + Wood

Wood feeds Fire. In the productive cycle, Wood's growth energy becomes your fuel. Their ideas ignite your enthusiasm. Their plans give your passion something to burn for. Their steady, directional energy provides the substance that keeps your flame from flickering out.

This is one of the most naturally energizing pairings you'll experience as a Fire person. And that's exactly why it's dangerous.

## The Chemistry

You feel it immediately with Wood people: a sense of being *fueled*. Where other elements might dampen your energy or fail to keep up, Wood gives you more. Their ambition feeds your enthusiasm. Their vision gives your fire a direction. Their growth-oriented presence makes you feel like you're part of something going somewhere.

Wood is drawn to your courage — the willingness to leap, to express, to be visible. Wood builds carefully; you light the match. Wood plans the structure; you bring the audience. Together, you're the pairing that makes things *happen*.

## The Friction

The energy flows one way: from Wood to you. That's the structural reality. Wood gives you fuel. You don't naturally give Wood what they need in return (Water — depth, emotional nourishment, being truly understood at a quiet level).

What you give Wood is excitement, visibility, and the thrill of seeing their ideas come alive through your expression. That's real and valuable — but it's not *nourishment*. Over time, Wood's reserves deplete. They give and give, and what they get back is your warmth — which feels good but doesn't replenish their specific hunger.

The pattern: Wood goes quiet. You notice the energy drop and respond by turning up your own intensity — "What's wrong? Let's talk! Let's do something!" This is exactly the wrong intervention. Wood doesn't need more stimulation. Wood needs to be asked about their own growth, their own plans, their own inner world — and then listened to in the patient, non-reactive way that doesn't come naturally to Fire.

## In Romance

**Early phase:** Electric. Wood's direction meets Fire's passion. You feel grounded without being constrained. Wood feels alive without being overwhelmed. The dates are an intoxicating mix of Wood's thoughtful planning and your spontaneous energy.

**Middle phase:** You start to dominate the emotional space without meaning to. Your feelings are louder, your needs more visible, your emotional weather more dramatic. Wood accommodates — because that's what Wood does with the element it feeds. But the accommodation has a cost.

**Long-term:** The question is whether you can learn to quiet your own flame enough to see Wood's needs. Not dampen yourself — just create space. "What do YOU need right now?" asked with genuine patience, is the sentence that saves this relationship.

**Survival guide:**
- Notice when Wood goes quiet. It's not a challenge for you to fix — it's a signal that they need attention to their own world.
- Celebrate Wood's growth explicitly. Not just the outcomes — the process, the ambition, the planning. That's Wood's love language.
- Give Wood regular solo time for their projects without taking it personally.

## In Friendship

The fun friendship. You and Wood friends have the best adventures — you bring the spark, they bring the plan. Together you're the pair everyone else wants to be around.

**Works when:** Both people have full, independent lives and come together to amplify. The friendship has energy, momentum, and a shared sense of going somewhere.

**Breaks when:** You start treating Wood as your emotional anchor during every crisis. The friend who listens at 2 AM, who absorbs your latest drama, who provides the steady presence after each of your explosions. Wood will hold you — but it costs them more than they show. And Wood won't tell you it costs, because feeding your flame is what Wood does instinctively. By the time Wood pulls back, the damage is done.

**The fix:** Check the balance monthly. How many conversations were about your life versus theirs? If you've dominated three in a row, the next one is theirs. "Tell me about your project. I want the full update." Then listen. Actually listen.

## At Work

**Partners:** Wood builds the engine, you generate the attention. The classic visionary-marketer combo. Wood creates the product; you sell it, promote it, and give it a face. This can build empires.

**The risk:** The credit gap. Your public charisma gets recognition. Wood's behind-the-scenes building doesn't. Over time, Wood resents the imbalance — especially if revenue is tied to visibility rather than creation.

**The solution:** Attribution. Name Wood's contributions publicly and specifically. "This product exists because of Wood's vision. I just turned the lights on." That sentence, said sincerely, maintains the partnership for decades.

**Wood boss:** Give them results and momentum. Don't bring personal drama into the professional space. Wood bosses separate growth from emotion — match that.

**You as boss:** Give Wood employees room to grow independently. Set the vision, then step back. Your enthusiasm is motivating until it becomes hovering. Trust their process.

## In Family

**Fire parent, Wood child:** Encourage without overwhelming. Wood children need space to grow at their own pace — not the pace your excitement sets. Your instinct is to push them into the spotlight. Their instinct is to grow in the direction they've chosen, which may not be the one you'd pick. Let them lead their own trajectory. Celebrate their choices, not just your projections.

**Wood parent, Fire child:** A warm, generative combination. Wood parent provides the structure and direction Fire needs while genuinely appreciating Fire's expressiveness. The risk: Wood parent channeling Fire child's energy toward Wood's own ambitions rather than the child's. Let the Fire child burn for what *they* care about, even if it seems impractical.

**Siblings:** Dynamic and occasionally rivalrous. Fire sibling gets attention for being entertaining. Wood sibling gets respect for being capable. Both forms of recognition matter. The sibling bond strengthens when both acknowledge what the other brings without competing.

## The Bottom Line

This pairing thrives when you remember one thing: **the person feeding your fire has needs that look nothing like yours.** Wood doesn't need excitement. Wood needs direction, space, and the quiet recognition that their steady growth is just as valuable as your bright flame.
