# Chapter 27: Special Formations (格局 & 神殺)

By now, you have a complete interpretive toolkit. You know the Day Master, the Five Elements, the Ten Gods, the Twelve Life Stages, and the three fortune cycles. You can read a chart from start to finish using the Seven-Step Framework from Chapter 22.

But Saju has layers beyond the fundamentals. Experienced practitioners look for *patterns* — higher-level structural configurations that emerge from the interplay of all eight characters. They also look for *mystical stars* — special markers derived from the relationships between specific stems and branches. And they pay close attention to the dynamic interactions between branches: clashes that create upheaval, harmonies that create flow, and combinations that transform one element into another.

This chapter introduces these advanced concepts. Think of them as additional lenses you can place over the chart you have already learned to read. They sharpen the image. They reveal details you might otherwise miss. But they never replace the fundamentals — they build on them.

---

## 27.1 What Are Special Formations?

A **Special Formation** — known as *Gyeokguk* (격국 格局) — is a structural pattern that describes the overall orientation of a chart. If the Day Master tells you *who* the person is, and the Useful God tells you *what the chart needs*, then the Gyeokguk tells you *how the chart is organized* — what kind of life architecture the eight characters create.

The concept is straightforward. Look at the Month Pillar's Heavenly Stem (or, in some schools, the dominant Ten God across the entire chart), and identify which god defines the chart's primary structure. The resulting formation names the chart's constitutional type — its default way of engaging with the world.

### How Formations Are Determined

The classical method examines the **Month Pillar's Heavenly Stem** and its relationship to the Day Master. Whatever Ten God that stem represents becomes the chart's formation. If the Month Stem is the Day Master's Direct Officer, the chart is classified as a Direct Officer Formation. If it is the Eating God, the chart is an Eating God Formation.

Some practitioners also look at which Ten God appears most frequently across all eight characters, especially when the Month Stem does not clearly dominate. The principle is the same: find the god that defines the chart's gravitational center, and name the formation after it.

> A formation is not a judgment — it is a description. A Direct Officer Formation is not inherently better than a Seven Killings Formation. Each has its strengths, its challenges, and its ideal conditions for success. The question is always: does this chart have what it needs (the Useful God) to make the formation work?

### The Key Formations

Here are seven of the most important formations, with their characteristics.

**1. Direct Officer Formation** — *Jeonggwan-gyeok* (정관격 正官格)

The Authority-Oriented Chart. The Direct Officer dominates the structure, creating a person whose life revolves around legitimate authority, social responsibility, and institutional achievement. These charts produce administrators, civil servants, lawyers, and leaders who thrive within established systems. The chart needs the Day Master to be strong enough to handle the authority pressure. If the Day Master is too weak, the structure becomes oppressive rather than empowering.

**2. Indirect Officer / Seven Killings Formation** — *Pyeongwan-gyeok / Chilsal-gyeok* (편관격 偏官格 / 칠살격 七殺格)

The Indirect Officer is also known as Seven Killings (칠살 七殺), a name derived from the fact that the element controlling your Day Master at the same polarity sits exactly seven positions away in the Sexagenary Cycle. We introduced the Indirect Officer in Chapter 12; here we use both terms interchangeably, as Korean practitioners do.

The Pressure-Forged Chart. The Indirect Officer (Seven Killings) dominates, creating a person who is constantly tested by harsh, unpredictable external forces. Unlike the Direct Officer's structured authority, the Seven Killings brings chaotic pressure — sudden challenges, power struggles, environments where survival depends on toughness. These charts produce military leaders, crisis managers, entrepreneurs in brutal markets, and anyone who thrives under pressure that would break others. The chart desperately needs a controlling mechanism, typically the Eating God or a strong Resource star, to channel the Seven Killings energy productively. Without it, the pressure destroys rather than forges.

**3. Eating God Formation** — *Siksin-gyeok* (식신격 食神格)

The Output-Oriented Chart. The Eating God dominates, creating a person whose life revolves around creation, production, and steady output. These charts produce artisans, chefs, engineers, content creators, and anyone whose identity is defined by what they make. The energy flows outward from the Day Master in a controlled, sustainable way. Unlike the Hurting Officer's explosive creativity, the Eating God formation produces with patience and consistency. The chart needs the Day Master to be strong enough to sustain the output, and ideally has Wealth stars to monetize what is created.

**4. Direct Wealth Formation** — *Jeongjae-gyeok* (정재격 正財格)

The Accumulation-Oriented Chart. The Direct Wealth dominates, creating a person focused on steady financial building, careful management of resources, and incremental growth. These charts produce accountants, investors, business owners who build slowly and sustainably, and anyone who views the world through the lens of value and return. The chart needs Authority or Resource stars to protect the wealth. Without them, the person accumulates but cannot defend what they have built.

**5. Indirect Seal Formation** — *Pyeonin-gyeok* (편인격 偏印格)

The Unconventional Learning Chart. The Indirect Seal dominates, creating a person drawn to alternative knowledge, spiritual pursuits, non-traditional education, and esoteric interests. Where the Direct Seal represents formal education and orthodox wisdom, the Indirect Seal represents the self-taught expert, the mystic, the researcher who follows questions nobody else is asking. These charts produce philosophers, alternative healers, independent scholars, and creative thinkers who learn by their own rules. The chart benefits from Output stars to channel the knowledge into something tangible. Without them, the learning becomes circular and isolated.

**6. Prime Formation** — *Geollog-gyeok* (건록격 建祿格)

The Self-Reliant Chart. This formation appears when the Day Master itself is the dominant force, specifically when the Day Branch contains the Day Master's Prime (건록) Life Stage. The person is constitutionally strong, independent, and self-starting. These charts produce self-made individuals who do not wait for permission or support. The strength is obvious in natural confidence and vitality. The risk is equally obvious in excessive self-reliance that refuses help even when help is needed. The chart benefits from Wealth and Output stars that give the strong Day Master something to do with all that energy.

**7. Rob Wealth Blade Formation** — *Yangin-gyeok* (양인격 陽刃格)

The Intense Competitive Chart. This formation appears when the Rob Wealth star dominates, specifically when the Day Branch contains the Day Master's Peak (제왕) energy and the Rob Wealth is prominent. The person has extraordinary drive, competitiveness, and intensity. These charts produce athletes, surgeons, trial lawyers, and anyone who excels in high-stakes environments where a split-second matters. The energy is sharp. Literally, 양인 means "Yang Blade." The chart absolutely needs the Direct Officer or Seven Killings to control this blade; without authority to channel the intensity, the person becomes reckless and self-destructive.

An Eating God Formation with a strong Day Master and good Wealth stars is a recipe for productive success. The same formation with a desperately weak Day Master is a recipe for burnout — the chart produces until the creator collapses.

Always return to the fundamentals. The formation tells you what kind of building this is. The Day Master and Useful God tell you whether the foundation can support it.

## 27.2 Key Mystical Stars

If formations describe the chart's architecture, Mystical Stars, known as *Shinsal* (신살 神殺), describe its hidden markers. These are special indicators derived from the mathematical relationships between specific stems and branches. They do not change the elemental composition of the chart. They do not alter the Ten Gods. But they add information that experienced practitioners find invaluable.

Think of Mystical Stars as annotations in the margin of a chart. The main text — Day Master, elements, Ten Gods, Useful God — tells the primary story. The annotations add context, nuance, and occasionally a plot twist.

### The Four Essential Mystical Stars

Dozens of Mystical Stars exist in the classical tradition, but four appear most frequently in professional readings and carry the most practical significance.

| English | Romanization | Korean + Hanja | Core Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nobleman Star | *Cheoneuil Gwilin* | 천을귀인 天乙貴人 | Help from influential people arrives at critical moments |
| Peach Blossom | *Dohwa* | 도화 桃花 | Charm, romantic attraction, artistic sensitivity |
| Traveling Horse | *Yeokma* | 역마 驛馬 | Movement, travel, career transitions, restlessness |
| Void/Empty | *Gongmang* | 공망 空亡 | Emptiness in a specific area, spiritual depth, potential loss |

Each star is determined by a specific calculation based on the Day Branch or Year Branch. The details of these calculations are beyond the scope of this chapter — they involve reference tables that practitioners memorize — but understanding what each star *means* is essential.

### Nobleman Star (천을귀인 天乙貴人)

The most auspicious Mystical Star. When the Nobleman Star appears in your chart, it indicates that help from powerful, influential people will arrive at critical junctures in your life. This is not vague "good luck" — it is specifically *people-luck*. The mentor who opens a door. The stranger who offers the right advice at the right time. The connection that transforms your career.

The Nobleman Star's position within the four pillars matters. In the Year Pillar, it indicates help from elders, family connections, and inherited social capital. In the Month Pillar, it indicates help through career and professional networks. In the Day Pillar, it indicates help through a spouse or close partner. In the Hour Pillar, it indicates help from subordinates, students, or younger people.

Charts with multiple Nobleman Stars are considered especially fortunate in social and professional contexts. Even during difficult fortune cycles, the Nobleman Star suggests that someone will appear to mitigate the damage.

### Peach Blossom (도화 桃花)

Named after the peach blossoms that symbolize beauty and romance in East Asian culture, this star indicates natural charm, attractiveness, and artistic sensibility. A person with the Peach Blossom does not necessarily pursue romance — they simply have an energy that draws others to them. In modern terms, think of charisma, stage presence, and aesthetic sensitivity.

The Peach Blossom has a dual nature. In its positive expression, it brings artistic talent, natural beauty, social grace, and success in creative or public-facing careers. In its shadow expression, it can produce excessive attachment to appearances, romantic entanglements, and difficulty with boundaries in relationships.

The determining factor is the chart's overall balance. A Peach Blossom in a well-structured chart with a strong Useful God enhances charm without creating chaos. The same star in a chart already overwhelmed by Wealth or Output energy can amplify romantic complications.

### Traveling Horse (역마 驛馬)

The Traveling Horse indicates movement — physical, professional, and psychological. Charts with this star produce people who are constantly in motion: frequent travelers, career-changers, immigrants, digital nomads, and anyone who cannot stay in one place (or one profession) for long.

Like the Peach Blossom, the Traveling Horse has two faces. In its positive expression, it brings adaptability, international success, diverse experiences, and the ability to thrive in new environments. In its shadow expression, it can produce restlessness, inability to commit, superficial engagement with places and people, and chronic dissatisfaction with the present.

The Traveling Horse becomes especially significant when activated by a fortune cycle. A person with this star in their natal chart who enters a Grand Fortune or Annual Fortune that also contains the Traveling Horse may experience a major relocation, career change, or life transition during that period.

### Void/Empty (공망 空亡)

The most misunderstood Mystical Star. Void does not mean "bad." It means *empty* — a specific area of life where the normal rules seem suspended. The position that falls into Void may experience loss or absence, but it may also experience spiritual depth, detachment from material concerns, and a capacity for transcendence that other positions lack.

The Void's meaning depends entirely on which pillar it occupies. A Year Pillar Void suggests detachment from family origins and feeling like an outsider in one's own lineage. A Month Pillar Void suggests an unconventional career path and difficulty with traditional workplace structures. A Day Pillar Void suggests complex marital dynamics and a spouse who is emotionally or physically distant. An Hour Pillar Void suggests an unusual relationship with children or a late-life orientation toward spiritual rather than material legacy.

Many accomplished spiritual practitioners, artists, and philosophers have the Void in prominent positions. The emptiness creates space — and what fills that space is up to the individual.

### A Word of Caution

Mystical Stars are the most frequently over-interpreted element in Saju. Beginners often latch onto them because they have dramatic names, but experienced practitioners treat them as modifiers, not main effects. A Peach Blossom in a chart with no Output stars produces subtle charm, not overwhelming magnetism. A Traveling Horse in a chart with four Companion stars and a strong Day Master produces occasional trips, not a nomadic lifestyle.

The stars amplify or color what is already in the chart. They do not create something from nothing. Always read the fundamentals first, then check the stars for additional texture.

## 27.3 Clashes, Harmonies, and Combinations

The Earthly Branches, the twelve animals that occupy the lower row of each pillar, do not sit quietly next to each other. They interact. Sometimes they clash, creating friction and upheaval. Sometimes they harmonize, creating flow and mutual support. Sometimes they combine, transforming into entirely new elemental energies.

These branch interactions are among the most dynamic forces in a chart. They explain why certain years feel seismically different from others, why some relationships click instantly while others generate constant friction, and why a chart that looks balanced on paper can feel turbulent in practice.

### Branch Clashes (지지충 / *Jijichung*)

A Branch Clash, *Chung* (충 沖), occurs when two branches that sit directly opposite each other on the twelve-branch circle meet in the same chart or fortune cycle. There are six clash pairs.

| Clash Pair | Elements | Nature of Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| 子 Rat ↔ 午 Horse | Water vs. Fire | Emotion vs. action, inner world vs. outer world |
| 丑 Ox ↔ 未 Goat | Earth vs. Earth | Stubbornness vs. stubbornness, values collision |
| 寅 Tiger ↔ 申 Monkey | Wood vs. Metal | Growth vs. cutting, expansion vs. contraction |
| 卯 Rabbit ↔ 酉 Rooster | Wood vs. Metal | Gentle creativity vs. sharp precision |
| 辰 Dragon ↔ 戌 Dog | Earth vs. Earth | Storage vs. storage, structural tension |
| 巳 Snake ↔ 亥 Pig | Fire vs. Water | Intensity vs. depth, visible vs. hidden |

Clashes are not automatically negative. They create energy through opposition, the same way friction creates heat or debate sharpens ideas. A clash between the Year Branch and the Day Branch can indicate tension between family expectations and personal identity. A clash between the Month Branch and the Hour Branch can indicate career-life balance struggles. The key is whether the chart has enough support (a strong Useful God, stabilizing elements) to channel the clash productively.

**When fortune cycles bring clashes**, the effects are acute. If your Day Branch is 子 (Rat) and an Annual Fortune brings 午 (Horse), that year activates a direct clash against your most personal pillar. This often manifests as relationship upheaval, health changes, or forced decisions about the direction of your life.

### Branch Harmonies — Six Harmony (지지합 / 육합)

A Six Harmony, *Yukhap* (육합 六合), occurs when two specific branches meet and create a bond. Unlike clashes, harmonies indicate attraction, cooperation, and mutual enhancement. There are six harmony pairs.

| Harmony Pair | Combined Element | Nature of Bond |
|---|---|---|
| 子 Rat + 丑 Ox | → Earth | Quiet loyalty, steady partnership |
| 寅 Tiger + 亥 Pig | → Wood | Creative growth, nurturing alliance |
| 卯 Rabbit + 戌 Dog | → Fire | Passionate connection, warmth through contrast |
| 辰 Dragon + 酉 Rooster | → Metal | Productive partnership, precision collaboration |
| 巳 Snake + 申 Monkey | → Water | Strategic alliance, intellectual synergy |
| 午 Horse + 未 Goat | → Fire/Earth | Warm companionship, emotional support |

When a harmony pair appears within a chart, say 子 in the Year Branch and 丑 in the Day Branch, it indicates a natural, built-in alliance between those life areas. When a harmony arrives through a fortune cycle, it signals a year (or decade) when a new alliance or partnership becomes available.

Harmonies can also *neutralize* clashes. If your chart has a 子-午 clash but a fortune cycle brings 丑 (which harmonizes with 子), the incoming 丑 partially calms the clash by bonding with 子 and redirecting its energy.

### Triple Harmony (삼합 / *Samhap*)

Triple Harmony, *Samhap* (삼합 三合), is the most powerful combination in branch interactions. Three specific branches unite to generate a concentrated elemental force.

| Triple Harmony Group | Branches | Combined Element |
|---|---|---|
| Wood Triple | 亥 Pig + 卯 Rabbit + 未 Goat | → Wood |
| Fire Triple | 寅 Tiger + 午 Horse + 戌 Dog | → Fire |
| Metal Triple | 巳 Snake + 酉 Rooster + 丑 Ox | → Metal |
| Water Triple | 申 Monkey + 子 Rat + 辰 Dragon | → Water |

When all three branches of a Triple Harmony appear in a chart, the resulting elemental energy is enormous, far stronger than any single element. If the combined element is the Useful God, this is one of the most favorable configurations possible. If the combined element is the Unfavorable God, it represents a deep structural challenge.

Even two of the three branches create a partial Triple Harmony, generating some combined energy. Practitioners call this a *half-combination* (반합), and it is activated when a fortune cycle supplies the missing third branch.

### Stem Harmonies (천간합 / *Cheonganhap*)

The Heavenly Stems also form harmony pairs, though the mechanism is different from branch interactions. There are five stem harmony pairs, and each produces a specific element when combined.

| Stem Pair | Combined Element |
|---|---|
| 甲 Yang Wood + 己 Yin Earth | → Earth |
| 乙 Yin Wood + 庚 Yang Metal | → Metal |
| 丙 Yang Fire + 辛 Yin Metal | → Water |
| 丁 Yin Fire + 壬 Yang Water | → Wood |
| 戊 Yang Earth + 癸 Yin Water | → Fire |

Stem harmonies indicate attraction and potential transformation. When two harmonizing stems appear in the same chart, or when a fortune cycle brings one partner to a stem already in the chart, the energy between them shifts. They do not simply coexist; they are drawn to each other, and under the right conditions, they merge into a new element entirely.

### Clashes and Harmonies in Practice: Charts B and F

Now let us examine how these interactions play out between two charts we have studied extensively.

**Chart B** (born 1996, Yin Wood): Stems are 丁-乙-丙-丙, Branches are 丑-未-申-子
**Chart F** (born 2001, Yang Metal): Stems are 丁-庚-癸-辛, Branches are 亥-寅-巳-巳

Stem Harmony: 乙-庚 (Yin Wood + Yang Metal, producing Metal)

Chart B's Day Master is 乙 (Yin Wood). Chart F's Day Master is 庚 (Yang Metal). These two stems form a natural harmony pair, 乙庚合, one of the five stem harmonies. When these two charts interact (whether as partners, colleagues, or friends), there is a natural *pull* between them. The vine (Yin Wood) wraps around the axe (Yang Metal). The result combines into Metal energy.

This is a fascinating dynamic. The harmony draws them together, but the combined element (Metal) strengthens Chart F's native element while transforming Chart B's native element into something entirely different. In relationship terms, Chart B may find herself adapting to Chart F's world more than the reverse. The attraction is real, but the transformation favors one side.

**Branch Clash: 丑-未 (Ox vs. Goat)**

Chart B has both 丑 (Ox, Hour Branch) and 未 (Goat, Day Branch) in her own natal chart. This is an internal clash — an Earth vs. Earth confrontation between her most personal pillar (Day) and her aspirational pillar (Hour). The tension plays out as a conflict between her present reality and her vision for the future. She does not need an external trigger for this friction; it is built into her chart.

**Branch Clash: 寅-申 (Tiger vs. Monkey)**

Chart B has 申 (Monkey) in the Month Branch. Chart F has 寅 (Tiger) in the Day Branch. When these two charts interact, this cross-chart clash activates — Wood (Tiger) versus Metal (Monkey), growth versus cutting. In relationship terms, this creates a dynamic where each person's energy structurally challenges the other in the career/social domain (Chart B's Month) versus the personal/identity domain (Chart F's Day).

**The combined picture:** Charts B and F experience both harmony (乙庚 stem attraction) and clash (寅申 branch friction) simultaneously. This is common in significant relationships — the people we are most drawn to are often the people who most challenge us. The harmony creates the bond; the clashes create the growth. Neither cancels the other. Both are real.

## 27.4 Special Consideration: Leap Month Births (윤달)

In the Korean and Chinese lunar calendar, a leap month, *Yundal* (윤달 閏月), is inserted approximately every three years to keep the lunar calendar aligned with the solar year. The solar year has approximately 365.25 days; the lunar year has approximately 354 days. Without periodic correction, the lunar calendar would gradually drift away from the seasons. The leap month is that correction.

For most people, the leap month is a calendrical curiosity. But for those *born* during a leap month, it carries specific implications for Saju reading — and has been a source of debate among practitioners for centuries.

### The Practitioner's Debate

The core question is deceptively simple. Which month pillar should be assigned to someone born in a leap month?

The lunar calendar's leap month is essentially a "repeated" month. For example, a leap 3rd month (윤달 3월) is inserted after the regular 3rd month. So which month governs the pillar — the regular 3rd month that just ended, or the leap 3rd month that is technically its extension?

The traditional school uses the preceding regular month's pillar. The leap month is considered an echo of the month it follows, not a separate entity. The alternative school uses the actual calendar position of the leap month, potentially blending energies from the preceding and following months. The modern consensus holds that most contemporary practitioners treat the leap month as part of the month it extends. A birth in leap 3rd month uses the 3rd month's pillar. However, they also acknowledge that leap month births may carry transitional energy, a quality of being "between" that does not appear in standard charts.

There is no universally settled answer. This is one of the areas where Saju remains genuinely ambiguous, and where the experience of the practitioner matters more than any textbook rule.

To illustrate the distinctive patterns that can appear in leap month charts, let us examine five real cases. These are not presented as evidence of any single theory but as data points that show the diversity and sometimes extreme nature of leap month charts.

### What Leap Month Charts Teach Us

These five profiles reveal several patterns worth noting.

First, extreme configurations appear more frequently. Identical pillars (Person 1), all-Yang charts (Person 2), doubled branches (Persons 3 and 4), and complete element absence (Person 4) are not unique to leap month births, but they appear with notable frequency. Whether this is statistical coincidence or reflects something about the transitional nature of leap month energy is debated.

Second, the "between" quality is real. Leap month births often produce charts that feel liminal, between two states, two directions, two identities. Person 1's identical pillars suggest a person folded in on themselves. Person 3's double Water pressure on a Fire Day Master creates someone caught between destruction and survival. Person 4's absence of Wood places the chart in a gap where the productive cycle skips a step.

Third, the Useful God becomes even more critical. In charts with extreme distributions, the Useful God is not just important. It is everything. Person 4 without Fire (Useful God) support is a candle in a Metal workshop. Person 5 without Metal (Useful God) is a raindrop under a landslide. The arrival of the Useful God through fortune cycles does not just improve these charts. It transforms them from struggle to function.

Fourth, professional guidance is especially valuable. If you were born in a leap month, the standard interpretive framework still applies — Day Master, Five Elements, Ten Gods, Useful God, fortune cycles. Nothing changes at the fundamental level. But the nuances of which month pillar to assign, how to weight the transitional energy, and how to navigate extreme chart configurations are areas where a professional practitioner's experience makes the greatest difference. This is not a limitation of the framework but a recognition that some charts require more skill to read well.

Fifth, leap months do not determine destiny. Being born in a leap month is not a disadvantage. It is a characteristic, like having an extreme Yin-Yang balance or a dominant Ten God. The chart still has a Day Master, a Useful God, and a fortune cycle that delivers opportunities and challenges in predictable patterns. The leap month adds complexity to the interpretation, not negativity to the outcome.

## Bringing It All Together

This chapter has introduced three layers of advanced Saju analysis. Formations name the chart's architecture, mystical stars annotate its hidden qualities, and branch interactions explain its internal and external dynamics. We also examined the special case of leap month births, where these advanced elements often appear in concentrated form.

The temptation with advanced material is to let it overshadow the fundamentals. Resist that temptation. A practitioner who knows every formation name but cannot identify the Useful God is like a literary critic who can name every rhetorical device but cannot tell you what a poem means. The advanced concepts matter — they genuinely do. But they matter *because* they add precision to a reading that is already grounded in the Day Master, the elemental balance, and the Ten Gods.

When you look at your own chart, start where you always start: Who is the Day Master? What does the chart need? What gods are present, absent, or dominant? Then — and only then — check for formations, mystical stars, and branch interactions. Let the fundamentals tell the story. Let the advanced concepts sharpen it.

That is the art of Saju at its deepest level: fundamentals first, always. Everything else is refinement.
